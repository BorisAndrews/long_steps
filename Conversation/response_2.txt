The paper you referenced, "An asymptotically optimal gradient algorithm for quadratic optimization with low computational cost" by Zhigljavsky, Pronzato, and Bukina (published in Optimization Letters, 2013), is indeed the foundational text for the ideas you are exploring.Below is an overview of the current state of the literature and the mathematical proof regarding the boundedness of your proposed error polynomial.Literature Overview: From Chebyshev to Arcsine SchedulesThe realization that gradient descent (GD) can achieve accelerated rates simply by varying step sizes has undergone a renaissance in the last three years (2023–2025).The Zhigljavsky-Pronzato Foundation (2012-2013):The article you suggested established that if inverse step sizes $h_k^{-1}$ are generated by a dynamical system whose ergodic distribution follows the arcsine density on the spectrum of the Hessian $A$, the asymptotic convergence rate of GD matches that of the conjugate gradient (CG) algorithm. This occurs because the arcsine distribution is the limit distribution of the roots of Chebyshev polynomials, which are the optimal error-reducing polynomials for quadratic problems.The Arcsine Stepsize Schedule (2024-2025):Recent work has formalized this as the "Arcsine Stepsize Schedule." It has been proven that for separable convex functions and quadratics, randomly sampling inverse step sizes from the arcsine distribution achieves the fully accelerated $O(1/n^2)$ rate. This is known as "stepsize hedging"—using long steps that are individually unstable but collectively optimal when balanced by short steps.The Silver Stepsize Schedule:A major deterministic breakthrough is the "Silver Stepsize Schedule," which uses a recursive, fractal-like sequence of steps based on the silver ratio $\rho = 1 + \sqrt{2}$. While the silver schedule is optimized for anytime convergence (remaining stable at every step $n$), your proposed Golden Ratio schedule is a sequential mechanism for sampling the same optimal arcsine distribution.
